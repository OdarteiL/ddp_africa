Electronics

Consumer electronics, components, and ICT equipment sold in the EU will require Digital Product Passports from 2029 — documenting material sourcing, repairability, and recycling instructions for every device.

Electronics and the Right to Repair

The electronics DPP is closely linked to the EU's Right to Repair agenda. The EU wants consumers to be able to repair their devices rather than replace them — and the DPP is the mechanism that makes repair information accessible. Every device sold in the EU must carry a passport that tells consumers, repair shops, and recyclers exactly what is inside it and how to fix or recycle it.

For African electronics manufacturers and component exporters, this means documenting supply chains that are often complex, multi-country, and involve both proprietary and commodity components.

What an Electronics DPP Must Contain

  • Material composition including critical raw materials and hazardous substances
  • Repairability score and spare parts availability information
  • Disassembly instructions for repair and end-of-life processing
  • Software update availability and expected support lifetime
  • Energy efficiency data and performance ratings
  • Recycled content percentage and recyclability information
  • Country of origin for key components and assembly
  • Conflict minerals documentation (tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold)

The African Electronics Sector

Africa's electronics manufacturing sector is growing rapidly — from smartphone assembly in Ethiopia and Rwanda to component manufacturing in South Africa and Morocco. African manufacturers supplying EU brands or exporting directly to European markets need to build DPP capability now, ahead of the 2029 deadline.

The conflict minerals documentation requirement is particularly relevant for African electronics manufacturers, given the continent's role in supplying tin, tantalum, tungsten, and gold. DPP.africa has specific expertise in helping manufacturers document responsible sourcing for these materials.
