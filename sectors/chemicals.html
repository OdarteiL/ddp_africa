Chemicals & Plastics

Chemical products and plastic materials entering the EU market will require Digital Product Passports from 2030 — documenting composition, hazardous substance content, and safe disposal pathways.

Chemicals and the REACH Connection

The chemicals DPP builds on the existing EU REACH regulation, which already requires manufacturers to document hazardous substances in their products. The DPP extends this to a full digital record — accessible via QR code — that covers the entire product lifecycle from raw material sourcing to end-of-life disposal.

For African chemical manufacturers and plastics producers, this means building digital documentation systems that can track substance composition, sourcing, and disposal guidance in a standardised, machine-readable format.

What a Chemicals DPP Must Contain

  • Full chemical composition including all substances above threshold concentrations
  • Substances of Very High Concern (SVHC) declaration
  • Raw material origin and sourcing documentation
  • Manufacturing process and location data
  • Safe handling, storage, and transport instructions
  • Recycled content percentage for plastic products
  • End-of-life disposal and recycling guidance
  • Carbon footprint across the production lifecycle

Planning Ahead for 2030

While the 2030 deadline gives more time than batteries or textiles, the complexity of chemicals DPP documentation means that preparation needs to start well in advance. Building the data infrastructure, supplier relationships, and internal processes required for chemicals compliance typically takes 18–24 months.

African chemical manufacturers who start now will be ahead of the compliance rush — and better positioned to demonstrate responsible sourcing and production to EU buyers who are already asking these questions.
